Particle physics data in your classroom?

 

MINERvA is a experiment at Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory studying the scattering of neutrinos from nuclei. MINERvA physicists and high school teachers from Rochester, New York, and Duluth, Minnesota, have developed exercises using MINERvA's data that tie into goals of the high school physics curriculum.
       
Measure the time that it takes muons stopping in the MINERvA detector to decay. Study a random process and measure properties, including half life, of radioactive decays.

Arachne is MINERvA's event display. In these exercises, a simple version of Arachne will allow the students to search for and analyze data from the MINERvA detector.

Supported browsers: Chrome, Firefox, Safari, but not Internet Explorer (sorry). Test Arachne on your computer by looking at live events from MINERvA.

  Resources for Your Classroom  

The primary resource document is a comprehensive teacher's guide for the set of exercises developed around the MINERvA data.

In addition, we have developed a menu-themed list of activities, each of which could be self contained. Or you could combine several into a set of activities for your classroom. "Appetizer" exercises are simple exercises that use the MINERvA data as a starting point for learning about elastic scattering and radioactive decays as observed in MINERvA's subatomic environment. "Main course" activities go further in depth on the topics and include opportunities for quantitative analysis of MINERvA data. "Dessert" activities are enrichment and conclusion exercises that follow on from the main quantitative activities and allow your students to learn more.
